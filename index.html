Module 1

A history of NLP

From language entropy to the Transformer, to agents, to world models.

Thesis: the "new" idea behind LLMs is old. Shannon framed language as a stochastic process in 1948. What changed was scale, data and architecture.

1948 - Language as a stochastic process

Claude Shannon models text as a source emitting symbols with probabilities.

Predicting the next symbol = reducing entropy.

$H = -\sum_i p_i \log_2 p_i$

Meaning becomes tractable when language has structure.

Sources: Shannon (1948) A Mathematical Theory of Communication; Shannon (1951) Prediction and Entropy of Printed English.

Make the link explicit: an LLM is a very large learned estimator of Shannon's next-symbol distribution. Everything else is engineering around this bet.

Three eras

Symbolic

Rules and grammars. Clear, brittle, hand-built.

Statistical

Counts, n-grams, word2vec. Data starts to beat rules.

Neural

RNNs, attention, Transformers. Representation is learned.

Sources: Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997); Mikolov et al. (2013); Bahdanau et al. (2015).

This is the orienting timeline. The key transition for non-CS researchers: from telling the machine rules to letting it infer representations from data.

2017 - the hinge

"Attention Is All You Need"

Self-attention lets every token weigh every other token in parallel.

Parallelism made scale practical; scale made capability visible.

AttentionTokens compare against the whole sequence, not only neighbours.
Feed-forwardThe model transforms that mixed context into new representations.
RepeatStack many layers, then predict the next token.

Source: Vaswani et al. (2017), Attention Is All You Need, NeurIPS.

Do not deep-dive the full encoder-decoder architecture here. Land the pivot: attention plus parallelism made large-scale training feasible.

2018 to today - the scaling era

The prediction substrate never changed. What emerged is a new level at which to read the system.

Sources: Devlin et al. (2018); Brown et al. (2020); Ouyang et al. (2022).

Tee up the levels slide: the base is still prediction, but "just prediction" is the wrong level to reason at. Then Module 2 opens the base layer itself.

Three levels, one machine

Prediction is the substrate, not the ceiling. The mistake is reading a system at the wrong level.

“A livello microscopico abbiamo i neuroni, a livello mesoscopico di mezzo abbiamo queste idee.” — Nello Cristianini (micro = neurons; meso = the learned ideas in between)

macro · exo Behaviour & judgement

The system read at a behavioural, almost psychological level (Dennett's stance) — and the human judgement that governs it.

Relate by: understanding, contesting, overriding.

meso · meso Learned concepts & competence

The abstract ideas the model built by itself (Cristianini's mesoscopic level) — the seat of reasoning, memory, skills and tools.

Relate by: steering with prose, context and tools — this is where you work.

micro · tropo Substrate

Neurons and weights computing Shannon's next-token probabilities. Always on, intrinsically statistical.

Relate by: knowing its failure modes — sampling, temperature, hallucination.

Keep Shannon at the base. Do your thinking — and your steering — at the meso level.

Sources: Cristianini (2024/2025), Machina Sapiens & interview (micro / meso / macro levels of machine cognition); Marr (1982) levels of analysis; Anderson (1972) More Is Different; Schaeffer et al. (2023) on the metric-sensitivity of “emergence”.

Cite Cristianini's mesoscopic level in one breath, then hand over to the atmosphere metaphor. His three levels: microscopic (neurons/connections), mesoscopic (the abstract concepts the model learned by itself, e.g. the "Golden Gate" feature), macroscopic (behaviour read almost like a psychology of machines, where he meets Dennett). Guard the flank: this is a claim about the right LEVEL of description (Marr), not that the model became a mind. Schaeffer shows "emergent abilities" can be a metric artefact - fine, because the argument is about level, not a magic switch. Every module from here answers: which level are we on? Module 2 = substrate; Modules 3-6 = meso system; Modules 7-8 = macro behaviour and judgement.

Module 2

Inside an LLM

Next-token prediction, tokens, and the probabilistic nature of every output.

Goal: demystify. By the end they should see an LLM as a sampler from a conditional distribution: powerful, but not magic, and not a database of facts.

One operation, repeated

An LLM computes a probability distribution over the next token:

$P(t_n \mid t_1, t_2, \dots, t_{n-1})$

It samples one token, appends it, and repeats.

Fluency is a loop, not a lookup.
The satellite detected
Hammer the loop. Hallucination, creativity and fluency all fall out of sampling from a distribution. Forward-reference temperature.

Tokens, not words or letters

The model sees sub-word pieces. That is why some "obvious" letter tasks are mechanically awkward.

Teaching-token view

Hover or tap a token to see why it was split.
token sub-word boundary

Widget note: this is a teaching tokenizer; exact splits depend on the model vocabulary.

Source: Sennrich et al. (2016), Byte-Pair Encoding for NMT.

Great myth-buster for a STEM crowd: the unit of perception is the token. Embeddings turn those token IDs into vectors.

From tokens to vectors

Each token maps to a high-dimensional embedding.

Geometry carries relationships: similar meanings sit nearby.

We meet embeddings again in Module 3 and Module 4.

Sources: Mikolov et al. (2013); Vaswani et al. (2017) section 3.4.

Plant the callback flag explicitly. Same vectors, three jobs: model input, specialised embedding models, and retrieval indexes.

Temperature: the determinism dial

Temperature reshapes the distribution before sampling:

  • Low T: sharp, stable, repetitive
  • High T: flatter, creative, riskier

$p_i = \frac{e^{z_i/T}}{\sum_j e^{z_j/T}}$

T = 0.7

Effect exaggerated for understanding the concept.

This is the high-value interactive moment: low versus high temperature on the same distribution. Keep the message simple: randomness is a setting, not a personality.

"Stochastic parrot" - useful provocation

The critique

Fluent form can invite us to over-attribute meaning.

The counter-view

Prediction at scale may learn structure that functions like understanding.

Use the phrase as a warning label: fluency is evidence of pattern mastery, not proof of grounded truth.

Present as a live scientific debate, not a settled verdict. Source: Bender, Gebru, McMillan-Major & Shmitchell (2021).

Hold the tension deliberately. The point is not to resolve the debate but to make the audience careful about anthropomorphising.

See the loop run

The model does not retrieve a finished answer. It builds text one token at a time.

Every word changes the next prediction because it becomes part of the model's new context.
Make the feedback loop explicit: generated text is not post-processing. The chosen token is fed back into the next step, so early choices steer later probabilities.

Module 3

Architectures

Two layers people conflate: what is inside the model vs. what is built around it.

The single most important conceptual correction: MoE and embeddings are inside the model; agents are around it. Most confusion lives on this boundary.

The boundary

Inside: processor

Weights, attention, embeddings, MoE routers and trained behaviours live inside the model.

Around: scaffold

Memory, tools, retrieval, policies and loops route work around a usually frozen model.

The test

If it changes parameters, it is inside. If it supplies context or actions, it is around.

Think of the LLM as the processor. The agent system is the motherboard that connects memory, tools and guardrails.
Give the concrete test: does it change the weights? MoE = yes, inside. Agent calling a calculator = no, around. This frame governs the rest of the module.

3a How a model is made

1. PretrainingPredict tokens over huge corpora. This builds general linguistic and world patterns.
2. Instruction tuningExamples teach the model to follow tasks as an assistant, not merely continue text.
3. Preference tuningHuman or automated feedback pushes outputs toward helpful, harmless, verifiable behaviour.

Embeddings live here too: specialised embedding models are later used for retrieval.

Sources: Brown et al. (2020); Ouyang et al. (2022); Christiano et al. (2017).

Non-CS researchers often think "the model" is one trained thing. Show the pipeline. Assistant behaviour comes mostly from post-training.

3a Attention & Mixture-of-Experts

Attention

Each token builds a weighted summary of other tokens in the same context.

Mixture-of-Experts

A router activates a small subset of expert blocks, increasing capacity without using all parameters every token.

Sources: Vaswani et al. (2017); Shazeer et al. (2017).

Emphasise MoE is an internal efficiency trick, not an agent or a committee of models talking to one another.

3a "Reasoning" - two senses

Trained reasoning

Post-training changes the system so deliberation is part of the model behaviour.

Prompted reasoning

A frozen model is wrapped with instructions, examples or loops that ask it to show steps.

Same surface behaviour, different mechanism.

Sources: Wei et al. (2022); Nye et al. (2021).

Keep inside/around discipline: trained reasoning is inside, prompted reasoning is a technique applied from outside.

3b Agents = LLM + loop + tools + memory

LoopDecide what to do next, observe the result, then decide again.
ToolsActions outside the model: search, files, code, databases, APIs.
MemoryState supplied around the model so the next call has useful context.

Patterns: ReAct, planning, reflection, multi-agent orchestration. Tools and memory get their own modules.

Sources: Yao et al. (2023) ReAct; Schick et al. (2023) Toolformer.

Land the payoff: the leap from chatbot to agent is mostly scaffolding, not a smarter model.

Module 4

Context & memory

The window, its limits, and how systems remember beyond it.

Core distinction: context is what is in the prompt right now; memory is what persists across turns or sessions. They are engineered differently.

The context window

Context is the model's immediate working memory: what is loaded for this call.

SystemRules and role instructions sit at the front of the working set.
History + RAGConversation and retrieved chunks compete for the same finite space.
Answer roomThe model also needs budget to produce the next tokens.
Bigger windows help, but they are not free.
Make tokens tangible: roughly three quarters of a word each. The window is working memory, wiped every call unless you re-supply it.

Long context: placement still matters

Lost in the Middle is about position-sensitive retrieval: evidence near the beginning or end of a long prompt is often used better than evidence buried in the middle.

A larger window gives more room, but it does not make all positions equally salient. Retrieval and ordering still matter.

GPT-32k tokens
about 1,500 words
GPT-48k / 32k
about 6k / 24k words
GPT-4 Turbo128k tokens
about 96k words
GPT-5.51M tokens
about 1.5 LOTR trilogies

Rule of thumb: 1 token is about 0.75 English words; the LOTR trilogy is roughly 480k words.

Sources: Liu et al. (2023/24), Lost in the Middle, TACL; Brown et al. (2020); OpenAI DevDay (2023); OpenAI model docs (accessed 2026-07-01).

Counter the "just use a huge window" reflex. Relevance curation beats dumping everything in.

RAG - retrieve, then generate

Query Embed Vector search Top-k chunks LLM Answer

Same embeddings from Module 2 - now used as a search index.

Sources: Lewis et al. (2020); Karpukhin et al. (2020).

RAG is the single most useful pattern this audience will actually build: retrieve the few relevant chunks, not the whole library.

Memory beyond the window

Short-term

Conversation history replayed each turn.

Long-term

Facts stored externally and retrieved when relevant.

Synthesis

Old turns compressed into compact notes.

Memory is architecture around the model, not a magical internal recall.
Summarisation is lossy. Flag the trade-off: forgetting versus overflow. Bridges to Module 5.

Module 5

Behaviour control

Steering an agent with natural language: instructions, skills, and tools.

Reframe programming for this audience: you shape behaviour mostly by writing prose, not code. Markdown is the substrate. Filenames vary by vendor; the principle is general.

Prose is the program

Behaviour is specified in layered, plain-language instruction files.

Layers compose: hard rules, project context, task persona.

RulesNon-negotiable constraints: safety, style, source policy.
ContextProject vocabulary, audience, data model, local conventions.
PersonaThe working posture: concise, sceptical, teacherly, playful.

Vendor flavours: CLAUDE.md, AGENTS.md, context.md, "soul" files. Same idea, different filenames.

Stress principle over brand. This is reviewable, version-controllable and human-readable.

Why Markdown?

Readable

Humans and models can parse it.

Diffable

Behaviour changes show up in git.

Structured

Headings and lists give salient cues.

# Rules
- cite primary sources
- never hide uncertainty

## Context
- audience: STEM researchers
- examples should be local

## Persona
- concise, warm, sceptical
# behaviour is now reviewable
For researchers: this is specification as documentation. The artefact that controls the agent is the same artefact a colleague reads to understand it.

Skills - packaged competence

A skill is a reusable capability package. It tells the agent when to load extra instructions, scripts and resources.

Structure

name, description, trigger rules, instructions, optional scripts/resources.

Triggered by intent

The description is the match surface: "debug this" loads diagnosis; "grill me" loads a critique flow.

Examples

grill-with-docs, caveman, personas, skill-creator.

Skills = modularity for behaviour. Good place to mention this very project uses skills.

Tools - actions the model can take

A tool is procedural: the agent fills a typed input, external code runs, then the result returns to context.

{
  "name": "read_pdf",
  "description": "Extract text and tables",
  "input_schema": {
    "file": "path",
    "pages": "range"
  }
}
FormatsPDF, XLSX, DOCX, PPTX, CSV, images, audio.
ResultParsed content returns as structured context the model can reason over.

Skills shape how the agent behaves. Tools give it procedures and file-format access.

The schema is the contract. Forward-reference MCP as the standard way to expose tools across systems.

Module 6

MCP & connectors

How an agent reaches the outside world: a standard plug, not bespoke wiring.

This answers "how does it touch my files, calendar or database?" Frame MCP as the USB-C of agent tooling: one protocol instead of N custom integrations.

The M x N problem

This is not a diagram about shared data. It is about a shared language format: a convention for how agents ask for work and receive results.

Without a conventionEach model/tool pair needs bespoke glue code.
With MCPAgents and servers speak the same request/response grammar.
ImportantMCP moves context and capability descriptions; it does not magically merge data stores.
Standard engineering motivation. Researchers will recognise the combinatorial explosion.

Model Context Protocol

An open standard where a host connects to servers that expose capability in a uniform way.

MCP standardises the envelope around tool calls, resources and prompt templates so an agent can discover what a server offers.

"open standard for connecting AI assistants"Anthropic announcement
"standardizes how applications provide context"MCP introduction

Sources: Anthropic MCP announcement; MCP introduction; MCP architecture.

Map back to Module 5: tools, resources and prompt templates can be served over a shared protocol and reused by compliant agents.

Connectors in practice

DiscoverThe connector advertises available tools, resources and schemas.
CallThe agent sends a structured request through the protocol.
ReturnThe connector returns results that become new context for the model.
Connectors are where a chatbot becomes a colleague that does things.

Same security questions as any integration: credentials, scope and trust.

End on the capability jump, but plant the risk seed: every connector is also an attack surface. Direct hand-off to Module 7.

Module 7

Security & risks

Three layers of failure: the model, the user, and the data.

Organise the module on three risk owners: model, user and data. This structure should stick even if they forget the individual examples.

model What the model does wrong

Hallucination

Fluent, confident, false.

Bias

Training-data skews carried into outputs.

Sycophancy

Agreeing with the user over being correct.

sampling training data feedback signals

All three trace back to Module 2: the model optimises plausible text, not truth.

Sources: Ji et al. (2023); Perez et al. (2022); Sharma et al. (2023).

Connect causally to the engine: hallucination is sampling from a distribution with no truth oracle. Manageable, but not eliminable.

user What happens to us

Cognitive delegation

Offloading thinking can erode the skill being offloaded.

Fluency trap

Polished output feels more accurate than it is.

Echo chamber risk

Recommendation and social systems can amplify familiar beliefs and misinformation.

Sources: Risko & Gilbert (2016); Sparrow, Liu & Wegner (2011); Lee & See (2004); Lee et al. (2025); Del Vicario et al. (2016); Cinelli et al. (2021).

Most relevant slide for an academic audience worried about students. The risk is not only a wrong answer; it is slow atrophy of the capacity to tell.

data Where data leaks

Cloud vs local

Who can see prompts and uploaded data?

Prompt injection

Untrusted text becomes an instruction.

Secret exposure

RAG corpora and credentials become targets.

The dangerous jump is when untrusted text stops being data and starts steering an action.

Sources: Greshake et al. (2023); OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications.

Drive home that connectors widen the attack surface: the agent now acts on untrusted input. Prompt injection has no clean fix yet; design for least privilege.

Module 8

Ethics

The human in the loop, who owns the data, and what happens to human capability.

Close the course on judgement, not tooling. These are deliberately open questions. Invite the room in rather than lecturing conclusions.

The human in the loop

Good loop safe

Human sets goals, reviews consequential action, holds responsibility.

Failed loop risk

Human as rubber stamp; automation complacency wins.

Oversight is meaningful only if a human can understand, contest and override.
Connect to automation complacency: oversight degrades precisely when the system is usually right. Accountability cannot be delegated to the model.

Data & power

Consent

Training corpora and attribution.

Concentration

Few firms control frontier compute.

Prompt data

Your questions are data too.

Present perspectives even-handedly. This is contested policy terrain, not a settled fact.

Labour & the human mind

Complement

AI extends reach: drafts, search, scale, translation between domains.

Substitute

Some cognitive labour is displaced. Which skills do we choose to keep?

build with judge well
The AI Translator builds with the tool while protecting the capacity to judge it.
Bring it home to the programme: the Translator is the human who keeps the loop meaningful.
